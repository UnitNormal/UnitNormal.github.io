An Atom Part 1: Schrödinger

This is not a complete course on concepts in quantum mechanics. We assume the audience had at least a semester in undergraduate quantum mechanics. The derivation of the wavefunction(s) along with many core concepts have been left out. There are many sources that develop quantum mechanic more fully than we will. We suggests Introduction to Quantum Mechanics by Griffiths or Modern Quantum Mechanics by Sakurai as starting resources on the subject. We will try to develop basic concepts to understand the visuals presented. The main goal is to visualize the single electron hydrogen atom in the framework of quantum mechanics as described by the Schrödinger equation and Dirac equation.

We will start by considering the simplest case. A single electron in the presence of a central nuclear potential. A non-relativistic electron in a spherical potential, namely the Coulomb potential, can be modeled using the Schrödinger equation

We can define the correction factor as M=1/(1+me/mN) and it is clear that M approaches unity for heavy nuclei. For a hydrogen atom, M≈0.99946. The index n,l,ml describe the eigenstate(s) of the Hamiltonian associated with the eigenvalue E. n is
typically referred to as the principal quantum number and uniquely determines the energy level of the atom, i.e. the eigenvalue E is only dependent on n, E → En. Solving for the energy in the Schrödinger equation yields

The principal quantum number takes on any positive integer values, n∈ℤ+. The orbital quantum number l describes the shape of the orbital. This is evident in the fact that both the radial and angular functions depend on l. In fact, there are l angular nodes and n-l-1 radial nodes. Of the l angular nodes, |ml| of the nodes lie in the x-y plane while the remaining l-|ml| nodes lie in the ρ-z plane. Values of l are non-negative integer up to and including n-1, l∈{0,1,…,n-1}. ml is the magnetic quantum number. It determines the shift in energy levels of atomic orbitals in the presence of an external magnetic field. Values of ml are from -l to +l in integer steps, ml∈{-l,-l+1,…,l-1,l}.

The charge of the nucleus does not affect the overall shape of the orbitals. The dependence on the charge, Z, is only in the radial function and energy eigenvalue. This means that the charge only affects how tightly bounded the electron is. The charge, Z, sets the scale size of the orbitals and the binding energy of the state. This is what we would expect given the Coulomb potential.

Electrons are fermions with spin 1/2. The quantum numbers s and ms describe the spin state of the electron. The solution to the Schrödinger equation with a static electric potential does not account for the spin nature of the electron. The spin states do not affect the electron probability distribution of pure eigenstates in the Schrödinger model in the absence of an external magnetic field. The inclusion of spin in our model is for the completeness of the quantum state.

The wavefunctions ψn,l,ml are energy eigenstates of the Hamiltonian for an electron in a Coulomb potential with energy eigenvalues given by En. Any normalized linear combinations of these eigenstates are valid solutions to the Hamiltonian and are valid states for the electron.

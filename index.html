Motion of the nucleus can be introduced by taking the reduced mass, μ=mNme/(mN+me), where mN is the mass of the nucleus, instead of the electron mass, me. We can define the correction factor as M=1/(1+me/mN) and it is clear that M approaches unity for heavy nuclei. For a hydrogen atom, M≈0.99946. For our purposes, it is sufficient to consider a stationary nucleus relative to the electron motion.

    The index n,l,ml describe the eigenstate(s) of the Hamiltonian associated with the eigenvalue E. n is typically referred to as the principal quantum number and uniquely determines the energy level of the atom, i.e. the eigenvalue E is only dependent on n, E → En. The principal quantum number takes on any positive integer values, n∈ℤ+.
    The orbital quantum number l describes the shape of the orbital. This is evident in the fact that both the radial and angular functions depend on l. In fact, there are l angular nodes and n-l-1 radial nodes. Of the l angular nodes, |ml| of the nodes lie in the x-y plane while the remaining l-|ml| nodes lie in the ρ-z plane. Values of l are non-negative integer up to and including n-1, l∈{0,1,...,n-1}. ml is the magnetic quantum number. It determines the shift in energy levels of atomic orbitals in the presence of an external magnetic field. Values of ml are from -l to +l in integer steps, ml∈{-l,-l+1,...,l-1,l}.
    The charge of the nucleus does not affect the overall shape of the orbitals. The dependence on the charge, Z, is only in the radial function and energy eigenvalue. This means that the charge only affects how tightly bounded the electron is. The charge, Z, sets the scale size of the orbitals and the binding energy of the state. This is what we would expect given the Coulomb potential.
    Electrons are fermions with spin 1/2. The quantum numbers s and ms describe the spin state of the electron.
